War, plague, inflation, recession risk, and, in the crypto scene, the narrative of stablecoin instability, the liquidation risk of crypto venture capital (VC), and so on—there is no longer anyone who insists this is a bull market, nor is there anyone who doubts that it is a bear market. We enjoyed the relative appreciation that came with the once-in-a-lifetime zero interest rates and the record-breaking Quantitative Easing (QE), and now, with the scheduled and record-breaking Quantitative Tightening (QT), we have set foot in territory we have never experienced before.

Rather than reciting to myself "this time will be different," at a time like this I threw out the fundamental question, "What has history repeated, and what should I do?" and was able to arrive quickly at the conclusion that it is a time for "selection and concentration." Right now, my selection is "buying Ethereum," and my concentration is building objective evidence for the question "Why?"

## 1. Protocol Revenue Comparison

![](18fe06-j4tJ8hFYFqeABn5VA9cH2A.png)

*(Protocol Revenue \| Source: Token Terminal)*

If you look at the chart above, over the past year, among Layer 1 (hereafter "L1") blockchain platforms, Ethereum's protocol revenue overwhelms all other L1 chains. What is called revenue in a blockchain protocol is fundamentally generated on the basis of network transaction fees and the number of transactions. The "number of transactions" refers to the number of transactions exchanged within the network.

![](18fe06-nNvq70WKp7T11J4nxsp0eg.png)

*(Ethereum transaction count vs. Ethereum price \| Source: Bitinfocharts)*

Beyond this, the number of transactions embodies many intrinsic factors such as the size of the ecosystem, the number of users, and preference for the blockchain. As the chart above shows, the movement of the coin's price and the number of transactions generated tend to move in tandem—to the extent that one could say "the value of a blockchain is proportional to the number of transactions."

## 2. Ethereum Burn Volume

Ethereum passed the EIP-1559 proposal through last year's London hard fork. The gist is that of the fees paid by users, about 70% is burned as the base fee, while the remaining 30% goes to the validators. Through this, as of July 21, 2022, roughly 2.55M worth of Ethereum has been burned to date, and in September of last year, the burn volume surpassed the issuance volume, resulting in a decrease in net issuance and confirming the possibility of a deflationary token.

![](18fe06-8EmG0BDWHMsHHtjkSf3-eA.png)

*(Ethereum burn volume \| Source: Watchtheburn.com)*

At the end of June 2022, through Ethereum's [Gray Glacier hard fork](https://blog.ethereum.org/2022/06/16/gray-glacier-announcement/), the Difficulty Bomb was postponed to around September 2022, which delayed the schedule of Ethereum 2.0's the Merge beyond expectations. However, the probability increased that, post-Merge, the burn from Ethereum's generated fees would exceed the issuance volume, making it a deflationary blockchain protocol.

## 3. Ethereum Issuance to Validators

Therefore, based on the reference site below, the issuance paid out to validators can be estimated at approximately 0.7M per year. In addition, the more the staking amount grows, the more the amount paid to validators decreases linearly.

## 4. Ethereum's Generated Network Fees

![](18fe06-WFSg7T6q6Zz-kFAeBnzEKQ.png)

*(Ethereum total transaction fees \| Source: glassnode)*

Ethereum's generated fees, starting from March 2020 when the Ethereum network became active, and taking into account the expected future decline in the current bear market, can be estimated at an average of about 160,000 ETH/month. If we take this as roughly 2M on an annual basis, the base fee burn volume (70%) due to EIP-1559 can be predicted at around 1.4M.

The validator's total revenue is the staking issuance payout (0.7M) + 30% of validator transaction revenue (0.6M) = about 1.3M, so we can expect the predicted burn volume above (1.4M) to overtake it. Because the calculation approach above is a simple projection based on the past, there is a possibility it will change depending on Ethereum's future scalability and narrative.

## 5. Closing

I am not trying to argue here whether Ethereum's price will rise on an Inflationary vs. Deflationary basis. It is rather a question of whether, at least at this point in time, there exists a blockchain platform among L1 blockchain protocols that can discuss such objective revenue at all. This is one of the reasons I look forward to the chain merge that brings us one step closer to Ethereum 2.0.

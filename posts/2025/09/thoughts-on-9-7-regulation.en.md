# **1. Overview**

I mainly cover crypto and Web 3.0, but even so, there is a market I would surely pay attention to if I lived in Korea: Korea's real estate market. Even while talking about a decentralized future, I cannot turn away from the main field of capitalism that operates most powerfully in the real world I live in. In Korea especially, real estate has served—beyond mere living space—as the single most important ladder for asset growth and class mobility.

🔗 [Tightening the 'Money Tap' Again After the June 27 Measures… Single-Homeowners' Lease-Deposit Loan Limit Capped at '200 Million Won'](https://www.newsworks.co.kr/news/articleView.html?idxno=810214&ref=wb3vb.io)

That is why, when a massive policy that seeks to change the market's paradigm—like the "Housing Supply Expansion and Market Stabilization Plan" announced this September 7—comes out, I have no choice but to examine it in depth. Some members of the public cheer at the enormous figure of 1.35 million units supplied in the greater Seoul metropolitan area, but among the experts and market participants who have looked into the policy's details, a cynical reaction dominates. I sensed that many people are placing great expectations on these measures. But first, I want to say, **"I'm not so sure."**

To state my conclusion up front, these measures are less an effective means of stabilizing the market and more **a manifesto that lays out a blueprint to ① reorganize the future real-estate market around public and rental housing, ② shrink the private sector's role, and ③ maximize the government's control.** I will discuss what the real intent hidden behind the plausible phrasing might be—from the relevant ministers' remarks to the policy's structural contradictions. In the end, it will become clear why the old proposition that **"apartments are not bread"** cannot help but be summoned once again.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# **2. The Hidden Intent Beneath the Surface**

The government's official announcements and opening remarks are the barometer that most clearly reveals a policy's direction. It is important to discern the intent hidden in a single word or a single sentence. The remarks of the Deputy Prime Minister for the Economy and the Minister of Land, Infrastructure and Transport clearly show the core of these measures.

Shifting the blame for the current market instability onto **the previous administration's deregulation, expectations of global rate cuts, and the opposition-led local governments' lifting of land-transaction-permit zones**; a perception that pats itself on the back for the effect of the June 27 measures even as the market does not agree; and remarks hinting at the additional designation of regulated zones—all of these undermine trust in the policy.

The Minister of Land, too, while admitting that the upward trend has not been broken, puts "public provision" and "the recapture of development gains" front and center as the solution. This is a highly anti-market approach in that there are no measures whatsoever for private supply, and it also tells us this is a structure in which all costs inevitably get passed on as taxes. Moreover, emphasizing "rental housing" as another axis of supply, and dismissing the cause of the market's problems as a matter of some illegal transactions, is—I believe—to turn away from the essential fact that the current market instability stems from the **"normal demand"** of the majority of citizens who want to buy their own homes.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# **3. The Measures' Rationale and Structural Limits**

Along with supply, the government pulled out its regulatory cards to suppress demand. But these are highly likely to miss the heart of the market and end up only tripping up innocent genuine end-users.

- **Strengthening LTV in regulated zones (50% → 40%)**
- **Ban on loans for home-trading/rental businesses (metropolitan area, regulated zones)**
- **Cutting single-homeowners' lease-deposit loan limit to 200 million won**

These regulations, which look powerful at first glance, are hard to make work properly in reality. Apartments in Seoul's major areas (the three Gangnam districts, Yongsan, etc.) already mostly exceed 1.5 billion won, so mortgage loans are fundamentally impossible to begin with. In other words, lowering the LTV to 40% **has almost no effect on speculative demand in the high-priced housing market.**

Rather, I believe the side that takes the real hit is the **single-home end-users who dream of moving up to a higher-tier district.** Cutting the lease-deposit loan limit results in kicking away their "housing ladder." Blocking loans for new rental businesses will also lead, over the long term, to the side effect of shrinking the private supply of lease-deposit housing. In the end, it amounts to constraining only the relocation freedom of ordinary households, not the speculative forces.

# **4. Supply That Is Only Numbers—All the Essentials Are Missing**

The core of these measures is, without question, "1.35 million units supplied in the greater Seoul metropolitan area." This works out to **270,000 units per year on average**, a scale equivalent to **building one new town every year**. But the success or failure of supply measures depends not on a simple number but on **① where (Location), ② when (Timing), and ③ how (How)** the supply is delivered.

These measures fail to answer all three of these key questions.

****① The Problem of Location****

What people outside Seoul, on the outskirts of Gyeonggi Province, want is not a mere "house" but **"a house in a place where they want to live."** Yet most of this supply plan is concentrated not in central Seoul, where demand is concentrated, but on public land-development sites on the outskirts of Gyeonggi Province. Concentrating supply in areas that lack job-housing proximity, school districts, and infrastructure fails to disperse Seoul's housing demand and only heightens the risk of unsold units. Supply cannot automatically become demand.

### **② The Problem of Timing**

Move-in with no set date. The previous administration announced the third-phase new towns, but as of 2024 the construction-start rate is a mere 6%. Most of the sites in the plan now announced—including the third-phase new towns—are ones for which we must wait until 2030, or even after 2035, for actual move-in. This is a story of far too distant a future to relieve the immediate supply shortage and anxious sentiment.

### **③ The Problem of Method**

Inefficient public-led delivery. The government put forward, as its alternative, a method in which LH directly implements the projects. But this is, in effect, closer to a structure in which LH takes on the projects that the private sector gave up on for lack of profitability. If LH—not a construction company—outsources all design and construction and performs only a management role, only the risk of inefficiency and defects grows. Considering the financial health of LH—which already carries 137 trillion won in debt (as of end-2023) and is projected to swell to 236 trillion won by 2028—I believe this ultimately amounts to nothing more than a game of "pass the bomb," plugging the holes with citizens' taxes.

# **5. The End of the Lease Deposit, and the Shift to a Tax-Revenue State**

The past June 27 measures and this September 7 package should be seen not as disconnected policies but as two pieces that complete a single big picture.

### **Stage 1 (June 27 Measures):**

- Aggregate loan-volume caps, conditional lease-deposit loan restrictions, a ban on mortgages for two-home owners, and a 600 million won mortgage cap
- These became the catalyst that sharply reduced the private supply of lease-deposit housing and rapidly shifted the market toward a monthly-rent structure. As a result, I believe they entrenched a decline in transaction volume and the preference for **"one solid property."**

### **Stage 2 (September 7 Measures):**

- Loan restrictions for single-homeowners, a shift to LH/public-led supply, and the expansion of public rental housing on the metropolitan outskirts
- The shift to public-led supply and the building of a tax base—these measures take a further step. By restricting even single-homeowners' loans, they shrink the freedom to relocate, and by putting LH front and center, they have made clear, I believe, the intent to have **the public sector completely take over leadership of supply.**

The final destination toward which these two policies head is clear, I believe. It is to **gradually extinguish the private lease-deposit market and shift to a monthly-rent-based, public-led market, securing both control and tax revenue at the same time.**

The government perceives the lease deposit as a "source of bubbles" that relies on leverage. It therefore seeks to extinguish it and shift to a monthly-rent structure. Behind this lies the practical purpose of securing tax revenue. Monthly rent is classified as business income that flows in steadily every month to the landlord (the property owner). Because this is a clear income stream, like a salary, its taxation becomes very simple and direct. Therefore, while it is hard to capture the tax base for deposit-form lease deposits, converting to monthly rent turns it into a certain source of income from which taxes can be secured steadily every month.

For now, the government says it "will not levy real-estate taxes," but I believe we should not take that statement at face value either. Even without directly raising tax rates, simply raising the fair-market-value ratio to 90% causes holding taxes to increase sharply. Through this, mid-to-large apartments in Gangnam might have to pay tens of millions of won a year in holding tax. The tax revenue secured this way is highly likely to be used as funding for the public rental business of LH—which is drowning in deficits—and for housing welfare, or as redistribution funds under the name of "monthly-rent vouchers."

# **6. Conclusion: What We Need Now**

Going forward, the current popular structure of the lease deposit is highly likely to shift gradually into a privilege that only the "cash-rich" can enjoy. With incomes stagnant, the rising share of monthly rent **erodes households' living expenses**, and an increasingly costly housing environment ends up threatening even long-term future planning. You might ask what's wrong with monthly rent, but monthly rent is a cost, not an asset.

What the market needs now, more than repeated announcements of supply figures, is **substantive deregulation that can revive the private sector's supply vitality.** We must boldly loosen supply-suppressing measures such as the reconstruction excess-profit recapture system, and create an environment in which the private sector can begin supplying **"right now"** in central Seoul, where demand exists. I believe these measures failed to strike that balance. The market does not move according to the government's plans.
